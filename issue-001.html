NAZA BUILDS.

He walked away from medicine because something louder was calling. Now he is building smart glasses from Aba.

NAZA BUILDS — Issue 001

The Profile · 5,200 words

The most socially validated career path in Nigeria is medicine. Your family understands it. Your community respects it. The ending is clear — Dr. Naza, done. He left it anyway.

There is a specific kind of person who hears something so loud inside themselves that it drowns out everything else. The family expectations. The sunk cost of years already spent. The safety of the path already paved. Naza is that kind of person. And what he heard, eventually, was the sound of a problem nobody was solving.

Safety in Africa is broken. Not in the abstract policy sense that gets written about in white papers. In the immediate, personal, everyday sense. The way a woman walking home at night has no recourse. The way an emergency can unfold with nobody knowing, nobody coming, nobody notified. Naza saw this not as a social problem but as an engineering problem. Which meant it had a solution. Which meant he had to build it.

"He left medicine not because he failed at it. He left because he heard something louder."

Jaga is what he built. An app-first safety platform — private circles, check-ins, a silent SOS that alerts your people without alerting the threat. And then the hardware: smart glasses that see what you see, a scrunchie with a hidden GSM chip, a tag the size of a coin that knows where you are. All of it built from Aba. Not Lagos. Aba.

That specificity matters. Aba is not where you are supposed to be building this. Aba is where you come from. Aba is the place people leave to go build somewhere else. Naza is building here on purpose. Because the problem he is solving is not a Lagos problem. It is not a Nairobi problem. It is everywhere, and Aba is as good a place as any to start — and better than most, because it is real.

He is twenty-something years old. He has a co-founder who believes in this as much as he does. He has a product that exists and works and is already in people's hands. And he has the specific quality that makes a founder worth documenting — he is not building this because it is a good business opportunity. He is building it because he cannot imagine not building it. The problem will not leave him alone. That is the difference.

The world was not watching. Docs Africa found him anyway. Now it will be.
